{\large \textbf{Computer Assignment 02:} \normalsize Obtaining a Numerical Solution to the Zero Pressure Gradient Laminar Boundary Layer Equations}

%DESCRIPTION OF THE PROBLEM
\section{Description of the Problem}

The objective of this project is to numerically solve the zero pressure gradient laminar boundary layer equations:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} = 0, \qquad u \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + v \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \left( \nu \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} \right)
\end{equation}

using the Crank-Nicolson algorithm to march in the x-direction, a stretched grid in the y-direction (where $\eta$ is the stretched coordinate that is uniform in the computational domain), and a fourth-order central difference scheme for the discretization of $\eta$ (except at the first interior points, where a second-order discretization is used). The following scales are considered: $\nu_\infty = 1.5 \times 10^{-6}$ m$^2$/s, $U_\infty = 30.0$ m/s, $L = 1.0$ m, $\delta = 0.005$ m. The initial velocity profile at $x = x_0$ is assumed to be:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{u}{U_\infty} = \begin{cases} \displaystyle \frac{y}{\delta} \left(\frac{3}{2} - \frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{y}{\delta} \right)^2 \right), & \text{for } \frac{y}{\delta} \leq 1 \\[4pt] 1, & \text{for } \frac{y}{\delta} > 1 \end{cases}
\end{equation}

Finally, the domain uses $N = 150$ grid points in the $y$-direction with $y_{\max} = 10\delta$, and the solution is marched up to $40L$ downstream from $x_0$. Further objectives of the project are to: estimate the initial value of $x$ based on the growth of the boundary layer, compare the results with the Blasius similarity solution, and calculate the displacement thickness, momentum thickness, and the shape factor in the $y$-direction.

%DESCRIPTION OF THE NUMERICAL METHOD
\section{Description of the Numerical Method}

To non-dimensionalize the system, one can define $u^* = u/U_\infty$, $v^* = v/(U_\infty \delta/L)$, $x^* = x/L$, $y^* = y/\delta$, and $\nu^* = \nu/\nu_\infty$. Substituting these non-dimensional variables into the original boundary layer equations yields:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial u^*}{\partial x^*} + \frac{\partial v^*}{\partial y^*} = 0, \qquad
    u^* \frac{\partial u^*}{\partial x^*} + v^* \frac{\partial u^*}{\partial y^*} = C \frac{\partial}{\partial y^*}\!\left(\nu^* \frac{\partial u^*}{\partial y^*}\right) \label{eq:nd_eqs}
\end{equation}

Where $C = (1/Re_L)(L/\delta)^2 = 0.002$ and $Re_L = U_\infty L/\nu_\infty = 2 \times 10^7$. A $\sinh$-based stretched grid clusters points near the wall. The physical coordinate $y$ is related to the numerical domain coordinate $\eta$ as follows:
\begin{equation}
    y = \frac{y_{max} \sinh(s \eta)}{\sinh(s)}
\end{equation}

Where $s = 10$ is a stretching factor and $\eta \in [0,1]$. The Jacobian and its derivative can be defined as:
\begin{equation}
    J^* = \frac{dy^*}{d\eta} = \frac{y_{\max}}{\delta} \cdot \frac{s \cosh(s\eta)}{\sinh(s)}, \qquad
    J^*_\eta = \frac{dJ^*}{d\eta} = \frac{y_{\max}}{\delta} \cdot \frac{s^2 \sinh(s\eta)}{\sinh(s)}
\end{equation}

Using the chain rule, the derivatives with respect to $y^*$ can now be expressed as derivatives with respect to $\eta$:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial}{\partial y^*} = \frac{1}{J^*} \frac{\partial}{\partial \eta}, \qquad
    \frac{\partial^2}{\partial y^{*2}} = \frac{1}{J^{*2}} \frac{\partial^2}{\partial \eta^2} - \frac{J^*_\eta}{J^{*3}} \frac{\partial}{\partial \eta} \label{eq:chain_rule}
\end{equation}

Therefore, expanding $\frac{\partial}{\partial y^*}\left(\nu^* \frac{\partial u^*}{\partial y^*}\right)$ in terms of $\eta$ and substituting the definitions above into the non-dimensionalized system yields the final non-dimensional system of equations in terms of  $\eta$:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial v^*}{\partial \eta} = -J^* \frac{\partial u^*}{\partial x^*}, \qquad u^* \frac{\partial u^*}{\partial x^*} + A_j \frac{\partial u^*}{\partial \eta} + B_j \frac{\partial^2 u^*}{\partial \eta^2} = 0
\end{equation}

where the coefficients $A_j$ and $B_j$ are defined as:
\begin{equation}
A_j = \frac{v^*_j}{J^*_j} - \frac{C}{J^{*2}_j}\frac{\partial \nu^*}{\partial \eta}\bigg|_j + \frac{C\,\nu^*_j\, J^*_{\eta,j}}{J^{*3}_j}, \qquad B_j = \frac{-C\,\nu^*_j}{J^{*2}_j}
\end{equation}

It is important to note that for laminar flow ($\nu^* = 1$, $\partial\nu^*/\partial\eta = 0$), so $A_j$ and $B_j$ reduce to $A_j = v^*_j/J^*_j + C\,J^*_{\eta,j}/J^{*3}_j$ and $B_j = -C/J^{*2}_j$. To discretize the continuity equation, the following 4th-order approximation for $\frac{\partial v^*}{\partial \eta}$ and 2nd-order approximation for $\frac{\partial u^*}{\partial x^*}$ are considered for the interior domain ($j=2$ to $j=N-3$):
\begin{equation}
    \left( \frac{\partial u^*}{\partial x^*} \right)^{i+1}_{j-\frac{1}{2}} = 0.5 \left( \frac{3 u_{j}^{*,i+1} - 4 u_{j}^{*,i} + u_{j}^{*,i-1}}{2 \Delta x^*} + \frac{3 u_{j-1}^{*,i+1} - 4 u_{j-1}^{*,i} + u_{j-1}^{*,i-1}}{2 \Delta x^*}  \right)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \left( \frac{\partial v^*}{\partial \eta} \right)^{i+1}_{j-\frac{1}{2}} = \frac{v_{j-2}^{*,i+1} -27 v_{j-1}^{*,i+1} + 27 v_{j}^{*,i+1} - v_{j+1}^{*,i+1}}{24 \Delta \eta}
\end{equation}

Substituting these approximations into the continuity equation and rearranging yields:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    &\frac{1}{24 \Delta \eta} v_{j-2}^{*,i+1} - \frac{27}{24 \Delta \eta} v_{j-1}^{*,i+1} + \frac{27}{24 \Delta \eta} v_{j}^{*,i+1} - \frac{1}{24 \Delta \eta} v_{j+1}^{*,i+1} = \\
    & - \left(J^* \right)^{i+1}_{j-\frac{1}{2}} \left[ \frac{0.5}{2 \Delta x^*} \left( 3u_{j}^{*,i+1} - 4 u_{j}^{*,i} + u_{j}^{*,i-1} + 3 u_{j-1}^{*,i+1} - 4 u_{j-1}^{*,i} + u_{j-1}^{*,i-1} \right) \right]
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Near the boundary and at the initial step in $x$ ($i=1$, $j=1$ and $j=N-2$), a 4th-order approximation is used for $\frac{\partial v^*}{\partial \eta}$ and 1st-order approximation is used for $\frac{\partial u^*}{\partial x^*}$:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    &\frac{1}{24 \Delta \eta} v_{j-2}^{*,i+1} - \frac{27}{24 \Delta \eta} v_{j-1}^{*,i+1} + \frac{27}{24 \Delta \eta} v_{j}^{*,i+1} - \frac{1}{24 \Delta \eta} v_{j+1}^{*,i+1} = \\
    & - \left(J^* \right)^{i+1}_{j-\frac{1}{2}} \left[ \frac{0.5}{\Delta x^*} \left( u_{j}^{*,i+1} - u_{j}^{*,i} + u_{j-1}^{*,i+1} - u_{j-1}^{*,i} \right) \right]
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Applying the Crank-Nicolson scheme and lagging the non-linear term $u^* \frac{\partial u^*}{\partial x^*}$ yields the following expression for the momentum equation:
\begin{equation}
    u_{j}^{*,i} \left( \frac{u_{j}^{*, i+1} - u_{j}^{*, i}}{\Delta x^*} \right) + \frac{A_j}{2} \left[ \left( \frac{\partial u^{*}}{\partial \eta} \right)_{i,j} + \left( \frac{\partial u^{*}}{\partial \eta} \right)_{i+1,j} \right] + \frac{B_j}{2} \left[ \left( \frac{\partial^2 u^{*}}{\partial \eta^2} \right)_{i,j} + \left( \frac{\partial^2 u^{*}}{\partial \eta^2} \right)_{i+1,j} \right] = 0
\end{equation}

The following 4th-order approximations are then considered for the interior domain:
\begin{equation}
    \left( \frac{\partial u^{*}}{\partial \eta} \right)_{i,j} = \frac{u_{j-2}^{*,i} - 8 u_{j-1}^{*,i} + 8 u_{j+1}^{*,i} - u_{j+2}^{*,i}}{12 \Delta \eta}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \left( \frac{\partial^2 u^{*}}{\partial \eta^2} \right)_{i,j} = \frac{-u_{j-2}^{*,i} + 16 u_{j-1}^{*,i} - 30 u_{j}^{*,i} + 16 u_{j+1}^{*,i} - u_{j+2}^{*,i}}{12 \Delta \eta^2}
\end{equation}

Substituting these approximations into Equation 13 and rearranging yields:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    &(\phi_1 - \phi_2)u_{j-2}^{*,i+1} + (-8\phi_1 + 16 \phi_2)u_{j-1}^{*,i+1} + (u_{j}^{*,i} / \Delta x^* - 30 \phi_2)u_{j}^{*,i+1} \\
    & + (8\phi_1 + 16 \phi_2)u_{j+1}^{*,i+1} + (-\phi_1 - \phi_2)u_{j+2}^{*,i+1} = (-\phi_1 + \phi_2)u_{j-2}^{*,i} + (8\phi_1 - 16 \phi_2)u_{j-1}^{*,i} \\
    & + (u_{j}^{*,i} / \Delta x^* + 30 \phi_2)u_{j}^{*,i} + (-8\phi_1 - 16 \phi_2)u_{j+1}^{*,i} + (\phi_1 + \phi_2)u_{j+2}^{*,i}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Where $\phi_1 = A_j/(24 \Delta \eta)$ and $\phi_2 = B_j/(24 \Delta \eta^2)$. Near the wall, the following 2nd-order approximations are used:
\begin{equation}
    \left( \frac{\partial u^{*}}{\partial \eta} \right)_{i,j} = \frac{u_{j+1}^{*,i} -  u_{j-1}^{*,i}}{2 \Delta \eta}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \left( \frac{\partial^2 u^{*}}{\partial \eta^2} \right)_{i,j} = \frac{u_{j+1}^{*,i} - 2 u_{j}^{*,i} + u_{j-1}^{*,i}}{\Delta \eta^2}
\end{equation}

Substituting these approximations into Equation 13 and rearranging yields:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    &(-\psi_1 + \psi_2)u_{j-1}^{*,i+1} + (u_{j}^{*,i} / \Delta x^* - 2 \psi_2)u_{j}^{*,i+1} + (\psi_1 + \psi_2)u_{j+1}^{*,i+1} = \\
    &(\psi_1 - \psi_2)u_{j-1}^{*,i} + (u_{j}^{*,i} / \Delta x^* + 2 \psi_2)u_{j}^{*,i} + (-\psi_1 - \psi_2)u_{j+1}^{*,i}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Where $\psi_1 = A_j/(4 \Delta \eta)$ and $\psi_2 = B_j/(2 \Delta \eta^2)$. The boundary conditions are known to be $u^{*}_0=0$ at the wall and $u^{*}_{N-1}=1$ at the freestream. Therefore for the 2nd-order stencil, at $j=1$, the $u_{j-1}^{*,i,i+1}$ terms vanish and at $j=N-2$, the $u_{j+1}^{*,i,i+1}$ terms become $(\psi_1 + \psi_2)$ and $(-\psi_1 - \psi_2)$. The former of the two is moved to the right-hand side, and they collectively become $-2(\psi_1 + \psi_2)$. For the 4th-order stencil, at $j=2$, the $u_{j-2}^{*,i,i+1}$ terms vanish and at $j=N-3$, the $u_{j+2}^{*,i,i+1}$ terms become $(-\phi_1 - \phi_2)$ and $(\phi_1 + \phi_2)$. Again, the former of the two is moved to the right-hand side, and they collectively become $2(\phi_1 + \phi_2)$. The numerical solver shown in Appendix~\ref{appendix-code} can now be programmed to solve the discretized system above and complete the aforementioned objectives of the project.

Assuming that the leading edge of the wall/plate is located at $x=0$, the initial value of $x$ can be expressed as $x_0 = \delta^2/c^2$. Therefore:
\begin{equation}
    x_0 = \frac{0.005^2}{\left(4.91 \sqrt{\frac{1.5 \times 10^{-6}}{30}}\right)^2} \approx 20.74 \text{ m}
\end{equation}

This results in $Re_{x_0} \approx 4.15 \times 10^8$. Furthermore, since $x^* = x/L$, the non-dimensionalized initial value of $x$ is found as $x_0^* = 20.74$.  The solution is then marched $40L$ downstream from $x_0^*$ to $x^*_{max} = 60.74$.

In terms of $\eta$, the non-dimensional displacement thickness can be expressed as:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\delta^*}{\delta} = \int_0^{1} \left( 1 - u^* \right) J^{*} d\eta
\end{equation}

the non-dimensional momentum thickness can be expressed as:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\theta}{\delta} = \int_0^{1} u^* \left( 1 - u^* \right) J^{*} d\eta
\end{equation}

and the shape factor as:
\begin{equation}
    H = \frac{\delta^*}{\theta}
\end{equation}

For the Blasius solution, the displacement thickness, momentum thickness, and shape factor are known to be:
\begin{equation}
\delta^*_{\text{Blas}} = 1.72 \sqrt{\frac{\nu x}{U_\infty}}, \qquad
\theta_{\text{Blas}} = 0.664 \sqrt{\frac{\nu x}{U_\infty}}, \qquad
H_{\text{Blas}} = \frac{1.72}{0.664} \approx 2.59
\end{equation}

\section{Discussion of Results}
\subsection{General description}

The numerical solution exhibits excellent agreement with the Blasius similarity solution at the downstream locations. Figure~\ref{fig:velocity_profiles} shows that the velocity profiles at $x^* = 25$, $30$, $40$, and $60.74$ closely match the corresponding Blasius profiles. From Figure~\ref{fig:velocity_profiles}, it can also be observed that the boundary layer grows as expected, and the velocity gradient at the wall decreases as $x^*$ increases. The similarity profiles in Figure~\ref{fig:similarity_profiles} highlight a key feature of a laminar boundary layer on a flat plate and self-similar solutions. When the velocity profiles at different $x^*$ locations are plotted against the Blasius similarity variable $\eta_B = y \sqrt{U_\infty/(\nu x)}$, they collapse onto a single curve (the Blasius solution). One can observe that the initial profile at $x^* = 20.74$ deviates the most from the Blasius solution. This is because the cubic approximation profile is not a solution to the governing equations, but rather an initial guess. However, subsequent velocity profiles converge to the Blasius solution as $x^*$ increases, and by $x^* = 60.74$, the numerical and Blasius solutions are nearly the same. It is important to note that accuracy improves further downstream in $x^*$ because the momentum equation is parabolic, therefore information is only propagated in the downstream direction and the impact of the initial disturbance (cubic profile) decreases as the solution marches forward. Table~\ref{tab:thicknesses} also demonstrates that the displacement thickness, momentum thickness, and shape factor all approach their corresponding Blasius values as $x^*$ increases. From the data, we can observe that the initial cubic profile overestimates the displacement thickness, momentum thickness, and shape factor compared to the Blasius solution. Nevertheless, by $x^* = 60.74$, the numerical $\delta^*/\delta$, $\theta/\delta$, and $H$ are within 0.6\%, 0.04\%, and 0.7\% of their respective Blasius values.

\subsection{Accuracy and stability}

The L2 residual shown in Figure~\ref{fig:residual_vs_x} decreases as $x^*$ increases, which indicates convergence of the solution. The initial residual is largest near $x^* = 20.74$ because the initial cubic condition must undergo rapid changes to satisfy the governing equations. The Root Mean Square (RMS) error between the numerical and Blasius solutions shown in Figure~\ref{fig:error_vs_x} also decreases (from a maximum of roughly 0.017 near $x^* = 20.74$ due to the initial cubic profile) as $x^*$ increases. Nevertheless, Figure~\ref{fig:error_vs_x} indicates high accuracy of the results over the entire computational domain. The accuracy of the solution is further confirmed by the differences in displacement thickness, momentum thickness, and shape factor listed above. Finally, given that neither the solutions nor the L2 residual show any signs of oscillations or unphysical behavior, the solution is stable. However, this is to be expected because the Crank-Nicolson scheme is unconditionally stable.
